What Is BMI and What Are Its Limitations?

Published 2026-01-05 • Motapa Calculator Health Guides

Body Mass Index, commonly known as BMI, is one of the most widely used screening tools for assessing whether a person's weight falls within a healthy range relative to their height. It was developed in the 19th century by a Belgian mathematician and has since become a standard reference point used by doctors, insurance companies, and fitness apps around the world.

How BMI Is Calculated

BMI is calculated by dividing your weight in kilograms by the square of your height in meters. The result places you into one of four broad categories: underweight, normal weight, overweight, or obese. Because the formula only requires two numbers, it has become a quick and inexpensive way to get a general sense of body composition at a population level.

Why BMI Is Useful

Despite its simplicity, BMI correlates reasonably well with body fat percentage across large groups of people. Public health researchers rely on it to track obesity trends over time, and doctors often use it as a starting point before ordering more detailed tests.

The Limitations You Should Know

BMI does not distinguish between muscle mass and fat mass. A muscular athlete and a sedentary person of the same height and weight will receive the identical BMI score, even though their actual health risks may be very different. BMI also does not account for where fat is stored in the body, and abdominal fat is generally considered more harmful than fat stored elsewhere. Age, sex, and ethnicity can further influence how accurately BMI reflects true health risk.

Using BMI the Right Way

The best approach is to treat BMI as one data point among several, alongside waist circumference, activity levels, blood pressure, and blood sugar readings. Use it as a starting conversation with a healthcare provider rather than a final verdict on your health.
